Uncertainty Quantification of Surrogate Models using Conformal Prediction

Vignesh Gopakumar, Ander Gray, Joel Oskarsson, Lorenzo Zanisi, Daniel Giles, Matt J. Kusner, Stanislas Pamela, Marc Peter Deisenroth

PDF Code

Machine learning surrogate models have revolutionised scientific computing—from weather forecasting to fusion reactor simulations. But there’s a catch: these models produce predictions without telling us how confident we should be in them. In safety-critical applications like nuclear fusion or extreme weather response, this lack of uncertainty quantification can have severe consequences. In our recent work, we demonstrate how conformal prediction (CP) provides statistically guaranteed error bars for surrogate models across diverse scientific domains, with near-zero computational cost.

The Problem with Surrogate Models

Data-driven surrogate models offer fast, inexpensive approximations to complex numerical simulations. Neural networks have become ubiquitous for solving partial differential equations (PDEs), weather forecasting, and plasma modelling. However, these models inherit uncertainty from multiple sources: the numerical codes they approximate, the training data distribution, and the model architecture itself.

The critical issue? Most surrogate models produce point predictions without any measure of confidence. When a weather model predicts a hurricane’s path or a fusion model predicts plasma behaviour, we need to know: how much should we trust this prediction?

While Bayesian methods can provide uncertainty estimates, they offer no statistical guarantees and struggle with high-dimensional spatio-temporal problems due to computational costs and dependence on prior specification. Deep ensembles require training multiple models. Monte Carlo dropout often severely underestimates uncertainty.

Conformal Prediction: Statistical Guarantees for Any Model

Conformal prediction offers an elegant solution. Given any pre-trained model \(\hat{f}\) and a desired coverage level \((1-\alpha)\), CP constructs prediction sets \(\mathcal{C}^{\alpha}\) that contain the true value with guaranteed probability:

\[\mathbb{P}(Y_{n+1} \in \mathcal{C}^{\alpha}) \geq 1 - \alpha\]

This guarantee holds regardless of model architecture, training procedure, or output dimensionality—requiring only that calibration samples are exchangeable with test samples.

How It Works

The inductive CP framework operates through three simple steps:

  1. Calibration: Compute nonconformity scores on held-out calibration data, quantifying the disagreement between predictions and ground truth
  2. Quantile Estimation: Find the \((1-\alpha)\)-quantile \(\hat{q}\) of these scores
  3. Prediction Sets: For any new input, construct intervals using the calibrated quantile

The CP framework: (1) Compute nonconformity scores from calibration predictions and targets. (2) Estimate the quantile for desired coverage. (3) Construct prediction sets by applying the quantile to test predictions.

Nonconformity Scores

We evaluate three approaches for computing nonconformity scores:

  • Conformalised Quantile Regression (CQR): Train models for the \(\alpha/2\) and \((1-\alpha/2)\) quantiles. The score measures distance to the nearest bound: \(s(x,y) = \max\{\underline{f}(x) - y, y - \overline{f}(x)\}\)

  • Absolute Error Residual (AER): Use any pre-trained deterministic model. Simply compute \(s(x,y) = |y - \hat{f}(x)|\). No architectural modifications needed.

  • Standard Deviation (STD): For probabilistic models outputting mean \(\mu(x)\) and standard deviation \(\sigma(x)\), use \(s(x,y) = \frac{|y - \mu(x)|}{\sigma(x)}\). This provides input-adaptive bounds.

Scaling to High-Dimensional Spatio-Temporal Data

A key contribution of our work is demonstrating that CP scales to the massive output dimensions typical of scientific surrogate models. We perform cell-wise calibration: computing separate quantiles for each spatial and temporal location in the output tensor.

Cell-wise uncertainty calibration for a U-Net modelling the wave equation. MC dropout produces unrealistically small uncertainties, while CP correctly identifies regions of high uncertainty.

Consider a model predicting the evolution of spatial fields \(\tilde{Y} = \hat{f}(X) \in \mathbb{R}^{T_{\text{out}} \times N_x \times N_y \times N_{\text{var}}}\). Our calibration procedure computes quantiles \(\hat{q} \in \mathbb{R}^{T_{\text{out}} \times N_x \times N_y \times N_{\text{var}}}\) in a point-wise manner, preserving the tensorial structure while providing marginal coverage guarantees at every location.

Computational Efficiency

The calibration procedure has complexity \(\mathcal{O}(d \cdot n_{\text{cal}} \log n_{\text{cal}})\), comprising element-wise score computation and sorting per dimension. Once calibrated, constructing prediction sets requires only \(\mathcal{O}(d)\) operations.

Experiments Across Scientific Domains

We validated our CP framework across diverse applications, from fundamental PDEs to operational systems:

PDE Surrogate Models

Poisson Equation: Our simplest test case—MLPs mapping initial field distributions to equilibrium states. All three nonconformity methods achieve guaranteed coverage with tight bounds.

Wave Equation: Using both U-Nets (feed-forward) and FNOs (autoregressive), we tested in-distribution and out-of-distribution scenarios. Even when testing at half the wave velocity used during training, CP provides valid error bars.

Prediction sets for the 2D wave equation. CP achieves valid coverage both within and outside the training distribution.

Navier-Stokes: Training an FNO on viscosity \(\nu=10^{-3}\) and testing on \(\nu=10^{-4}\), uncalibrated MC dropout provided only 7.52% coverage—severely underestimating errors. CP calibration corrects this to achieve the target coverage.

Magnetohydrodynamics for Fusion

We applied CP to a reduced-MHD system modelling plasma evolution in tokamaks, predicting three coupled variables—density, electrostatic potential, and temperature—on a toroidal grid. This represents a challenging multi-variable, multi-physics problem with 1.35 million output dimensions.

We also demonstrate CP’s value for foundation model deployment: calibrating a pre-trained Multi-Physics Pretrained (MPP) vision transformer fine-tuned on fusion data. CP provides valid uncertainty bounds without any retraining of the expensive foundation model.

Weather Forecasting

We deployed CP on graph neural network weather models operating at two scales:

  • Limited Area Model: 20.6 million dimensions over the Nordic region
  • Global Model: 12.8 million dimensions at 1° resolution

CP achieved valid coverage across all meteorological variables and lead times, with calibration completing in minutes on standard hardware.

Empirical coverage for weather prediction across different lead times. CP maintains guaranteed coverage even as forecasts extend further into the future.

Out-of-Distribution Robustness

A critical finding: CP provides valid prediction sets even when deployed outside the training distribution, provided exchangeability holds between calibration and prediction regimes.

This enables valuable uncertainty quantification for previously unseen solution families. When a fusion model trained on one plasma configuration encounters different operating conditions, or when a weather model faces an unusual meteorological pattern, CP can still provide guaranteed bounds—as long as calibration data represents the deployment distribution.

CP performance on out-of-distribution data. Valid coverage is maintained even when physics parameters differ from training.

Limitations and Practical Considerations

While CP offers substantial benefits, several limitations must be acknowledged:

Marginal vs. Conditional Coverage: The guarantee provides validity averaged over all predictions, not for each individual prediction. In risk-critical scenarios requiring reliable bounds for specific predictions, marginal coverage may be insufficient.

Exchangeability Requirements: CP requires calibration data that is exchangeable with the prediction regime. For experimental data where distribution shifts are common, violations lead to invalid coverage.

Input Independence in Deterministic Models: For AER scores, error bars are fixed during calibration and don’t vary with inputs. Probabilistic models (STD scores) provide input-adaptive bounds.

Cell-wise Independence: Our framework treats each spatio-temporal point independently, not capturing correlation structures inherent in physical systems.

Practical Recommendations

For optimal results with CP:

  1. Validate exchangeability assumptions through sensitivity analyses
  2. Use conservative \(\alpha\) values for risk-critical applications
  3. Prefer probabilistic models when input-dependent bounds are crucial
  4. Maintain awareness of marginal coverage guarantees when interpreting individual predictions
  5. Calibration data size: \(n_{\text{cal}} \geq 1000\) typically provides reliable coverage

Conclusion

Conformal prediction provides a practical, computationally efficient method for uncertainty quantification in scientific machine learning. Our framework:

  • Provides statistical guarantees regardless of model architecture, training regime, or output dimensionality
  • Scales to 20+ million dimensions with calibration times of minutes on standard hardware
  • Enables validation of pre-trained models for specific downstream applications without retraining
  • Works out-of-distribution when calibration matches the deployment regime

For practitioners deploying surrogate models in safety-critical contexts—from fusion reactor control to extreme weather response—CP offers actionable uncertainty quantification where uncertainty matters most.
